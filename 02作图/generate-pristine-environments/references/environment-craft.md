# Environment craft

## Spatial design

- Establish one dominant mass, one supporting mass, and meaningful negative space.
- Build depth through overlap, relative scale, value compression, texture falloff, and atmosphere—not blur alone.
- Give foreground elements a compositional job: frame, lead, measure scale, or reveal material. Avoid random foreground clutter.
- Keep the focal route readable. Roads, light, walls, rivers, stairs, canopy openings, or shadow edges can direct attention.
- Use asymmetry with balance. Centered symmetry is appropriate for ceremonial, axial, or monumental spaces, not a universal default.

## Architecture and interiors

- Define function before style. A believable building needs entrance, circulation, support, enclosure, drainage, and plausible module sizes.
- Preserve construction hierarchy: foundation, primary frame, secondary frame, enclosure, openings, trim, and ornament.
- Make repeated bays, windows, columns, stairs, tiles, and railings consistent in spacing and scale.
- In interiors, resolve floor-wall-ceiling junctions, door clearance, furniture scale, light fixtures, and usable pathways.
- Ornament follows structure and culture; it does not replace structure.
- Apply an ornament limit: name the exact zones that receive carving, relief, trim, signage, or decorative pattern. Preserve broad undecorated structural planes elsewhere. Never distribute micro-ornament uniformly across every surface.

## Natural environments

- Let geology determine slope, erosion, water path, soil exposure, and vegetation density.
- Group vegetation by species, moisture, elevation, wind, light, and succession instead of scattering identical plants.
- Water needs a source, basin or channel, contact edges, reflection appropriate to roughness, and consistent gravity.
- Clouds, mist, snow, dust, and rain must agree with wind, temperature, distance, and lighting.

## Materials and weathering

- Describe each major material by base substance, finish, roughness, scale, edge behavior, contact behavior, and localized age.
- Put wear where forces act: thresholds, hand height, runoff paths, exposed edges, joints, wheel tracks, splash zones, and sun-facing planes.
- Avoid uniform dirt, procedural scratches everywhere, identical cracks, and wetness on every surface.
- Separate stone, plaster, timber, metal, glass, fabric, soil, foliage, and water through response to light—not color alone.
- Use one primary weathering anchor per major material, then specify its distribution. Prefer “oxidation collected in recessed joints while exposed faces remain clean” over stacking `aged`, `weathered`, `worn`, `dirty`, and `patinated` across the whole scene.

## Lighting and atmosphere

- Use one dominant physical source; secondary sources must have credible position and strength.
- Maintain shadow direction and softness across the scene. Contact shadows ground objects.
- Use bounce appropriate to nearby surfaces and sky color.
- Reserve the highest contrast and clearest color separation for the focal zone.
- Atmosphere lowers contrast, saturation, and detail with distance. It should not uniformly whiten the frame.
- Give dark regions a controlled hue and continuous directional gradient rather than pure black or lifted gray. Preserve only structure-bearing information in shadows; do not add arbitrary objects merely to make dark areas busy.

## Detail budget

- Primary detail zone: landmark, entrance, hero interior area, or key natural formation.
- Optional secondary zone: foreground material anchor or supporting structure.
- Keep broad sky, ground, wall, water, or distant terrain quieter.
- Vast shots require macro detail; close architectural views can support joints, grain, seams, and surface wear.
